Ramen: A Brief History

Ramen is a Japanese dish that has become popular all over the world. It consists of Chinese-style wheat noodles served in a meat or fish-based broth, often flavored with soy sauce or miso, and topped with ingredients such as sliced pork, seaweed, and green onions.

The origins of ramen can be traced back to China, where wheat noodles were a staple food. In the early 20th century, Japanese soldiers returning from China brought back this dish and it became popular in Japan. Over time, ramen evolved into a unique Japanese dish with its own distinctive flavors and ingredients.

Today, ramen is enjoyed by people all over the world, and has even inspired new variations and fusion dishes. Whether you prefer a classic bowl of shoyu ramen, a spicy miso ramen, or a vegetarian version, there's a type of ramen out there for everyone.

Tonkotsu Ramen

A rich and creamy pork-based broth served with thin noodles, sliced pork, and green onions.

Shoyu Ramen

A soy sauce-based broth served with thin noodles, sliced pork, and bamboo shoots.

Shio Ramen

A salt-based broth served with thin noodles, sliced pork, and seaweed.

Miso Ramen

A miso-based broth served with thin noodles, sliced pork, and bean sprouts.

Tsukemen

A dipping-style ramen where the noodles and toppings are served separately from a concentrated broth for dipping.

Tantanmen

A spicy ramen with a sesame and soy sauce-based broth served with ground pork, green onions, and bok choy.

Chashumen

A ramen with a soy sauce-based broth served with thick noodles, sliced pork, and vegetables.

Curry Ramen

A ramen with a curry-based broth served with thin noodles, sliced pork, and carrots.

Seafood Ramen

A ramen with a seafood-based broth served with thin noodles, shrimp, squid, and mussels.

Vegetable Ramen

A ramen with a vegetable-based broth served with thin noodles, mushrooms, and bok choy.
